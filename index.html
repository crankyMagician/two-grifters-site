The Count Who Sold the Eiffel Tower. Twice.

Episode 3 · July 20, 2026 · 17:38

In the spring of 1925, a man with printed letterhead and a borrowed title invited Paris's biggest scrap dealers to a grand hotel and sold one of them the Eiffel Tower. Nobody reported it, so he came back and sold it again. The story of Count Victor Lustig: the money box, the tower, the counterfeit hundreds that spooked the Treasury, and a daylight escape down the side of a New York jail.

Sources
  • Jeff Maysh, 'The Man Who Sold the Eiffel Tower. Twice.', Smithsonian Magazine, March 2016
  • James F. Johnson and Floyd Miller, 'The Man Who Sold the Eiffel Tower,' Doubleday, 1961
  • United States Penitentiary, Alcatraz, inmate records for register no. 300 (Robert V. Miller, alias Victor Lustig), U.S. National Archives
  • New York Times reporting on the September 1935 escape from the Federal House of Detention and the Pittsburgh recapture
  • La Tour Eiffel official site, history section: 'Who sold the Eiffel Tower?'

The Great Train Robbery: Undone by a Monopoly Board

Episode 2 · July 20, 2026 · 35:02

In August 1963, fifteen men stopped the Glasgow to London mail train at a rigged red signal in the Buckinghamshire countryside and unloaded 2.6 million pounds in used banknotes in under half an hour. No guns fired, no alarm raised, the biggest cash robbery Britain had ever seen. Then they holed up in a farmhouse to wait out the manhunt, and somebody dealt out a game of Monopoly.

Sources
  • Piers Paul Read, 'The Train Robbers,' W.H. Allen, 1978
  • Bruce Reynolds, 'The Autobiography of a Thief,' Bantam Press, 1995
  • Nick Russell-Pavier and Stewart Richards, 'The Great Train Robbery: Crime of the Century,' Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 2013
  • The National Archives (UK), case files on the 1963 Cheddington mail train robbery, DPP 2/3715 series and HO 287/1496
  • BBC News coverage of the robbery's fiftieth anniversary and the December 2013 obituary of Ronnie Biggs

The Antwerp Diamond Heist: Beaten by a Sandwich

Episode 1 · July 20, 2026 · 12:27

In February 2003, a crew of Italian thieves emptied 109 safe deposit boxes beneath the most secure building in the world's diamond capital, without tripping a single alarm. They beat a three-ton vault door, seismic sensors, and a lock with one hundred million combinations. Then a half-eaten salami sandwich brought the whole thing down.

Sources
  • Joshua Davis, 'The Untold Story of the World's Biggest Diamond Heist,' Wired, March 2009: https://www.wired.com/2009/03/ff-diamonds-2/
  • Scott Andrew Selby and Greg Campbell, 'Flawless: Inside the Largest Diamond Heist in History,' Union Square Press, 2010
  • BBC News coverage of the February 2003 Antwerp Diamond Centre theft and the 2005 trial of Leonardo Notarbartolo
